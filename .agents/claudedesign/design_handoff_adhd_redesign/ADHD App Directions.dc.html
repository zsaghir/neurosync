Cognitive-load tradeoffs

1a Warm & Editorial — the serif voice and rule-line lists make the app feel personal and low-pressure, and grouping by rule instead of boxes keeps density down. Tradeoff: the richer type hierarchy (serif headline + sans body + italic asides) asks a reader to parse more visual registers than B or C — good for someone who enjoys texture, harder for someone who wants zero styling to parse.

1b Calm & Softly Structured — flat tinted blocks give every item a soft, consistent visual boundary without shadows or borders, which is easy to scan and feels supportive rather than clinical. Tradeoff: more surface area is "designed" (rounded blocks everywhere), so it carries slightly more visual weight per screen than 1c, and the sage/blue tinting needs restraint or it starts to read busy.

1c Extremely Minimal — the lowest moment-to-moment load: one task foregrounded on Today, hairline-only lists, a single accent used once per screen. Best for reducing decision fatigue and overwhelm in the moment. Tradeoff: it hides more behind taps (notes, subtasks review, session history), which costs a bit more navigation for a user who wants everything visible at once, and offers less warmth/personality than A or B.

Recommendation: 1c for the core task/timer loop (Today, Tasks, Add, Details, Timer, Review) — those are the highest-frequency, highest-distraction-risk screens, where minimal chrome matters most. Pair it with 1b's softer, flat-block treatment for Time Map and Settings, which are lower-frequency and benefit from a bit more visual warmth once the user is in a reflective, less time-pressured mode. Avoid mixing in 1a's serif system, since a second typographic voice adds load without a clear payoff here.
